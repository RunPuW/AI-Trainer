# Squat Biomechanics

## Joint Kinetics

The squat is a compound, multi-joint exercise primarily involving the knee, hip, and ankle joints. The relative contribution of each joint depends on squat depth, stance width, and bar placement.

### Knee Joint
- Peak knee flexion during a full squat typically reaches 120-140 degrees.
- Knee extensor moment increases progressively with depth, peaking at maximum flexion.
- The patellofemoral joint stress increases significantly below 90 degrees of flexion.

### Hip Joint
- Hip extensor moment is the primary driver during the ascent phase.
- A wider stance increases hip adduction and external rotation moments.
- Hip flexion range of 90-130 degrees is typical in a full squat.

### Ankle Joint
- Ankle dorsiflexion mobility directly affects squat depth and trunk position.
- Limited dorsiflexion (< 30 degrees) often leads to compensatory forward lean.
- Heel elevation can partially compensate for ankle mobility restrictions.

## Muscle Activation

| Phase | Primary Muscles | Secondary Muscles |
|-------|----------------|-------------------|
| Descent | Eccentric quadriceps | Hamstrings, gluteus maximus |
| Bottom | Gluteus maximus, adductors | Quadriceps, erector spinae |
| Ascent | Quadriceps, gluteus maximus | Hamstrings, calves, core |

## Squat Depth Standards

- **Quarter squat**: Knee angle > 120 degrees (partial range)
- **Half squat**: Knee angle 90-120 degrees
- **Parallel squat**: Thigh parallel to ground, knee angle ~90 degrees
- **Deep squat**: Knee angle < 90 degrees (full range)

# Knee Valgus During Squatting

## Definition

Knee valgus (also called "knee cave") is the inward collapse of the knee toward the midline during weight-bearing activities. It is one of the most common squat faults observed in both novice and experienced lifters.

## Biomechanical Analysis

### Normal Range
- Minor valgus deviation (5-10 degrees) during the eccentric phase is biomechanically normal.
- The knee should track over the second toe throughout the movement.
- A knee-to-foot-width ratio > 0.85 indicates acceptable alignment.

### Problematic Valgus
- Dynamic valgus > 15 degrees increases ACL loading by 2-3x.
- Repetitive valgus under load contributes to patellofemoral pain syndrome.
- The ratio of knee width to foot width dropping below 0.85 indicates significant valgus.

## Contributing Factors

1. **Weak hip abductors** (gluteus medius) - cannot control femoral internal rotation
2. **Weak hip external rotators** - insufficient control of femoral alignment
3. **Tight hip adductors** - restricts the knee from tracking outward
4. **Poor ankle dorsiflexion** - compensatory pronation leads to tibial internal rotation
5. **Motor control deficit** - lack of proprioceptive awareness of knee position

## Corrective Strategies

### Immediate Cues
- "Push your knees out over your toes"
- "Spread the floor apart with your feet"
- "Screw your feet into the ground"

### Strengthening Exercises
- Clamshells with resistance band
- Side-lying hip abduction
- Monster walks (lateral band walks)
- Single-leg Romanian deadlifts
- Copenhagen adductor exercises

### Mobility Work
- Hip adductor foam rolling
- 90/90 hip switches
- Ankle dorsiflexion stretches
- Deep squat holds with band distraction

## Monitoring

Real-time feedback on knee valgus can be provided using:
- Video analysis with angle measurement
- Wearable sensors (IMUs on the knee)
- Markerless pose estimation (MediaPipe, OpenPose)
